Introduction to Italy

Italy became a nation-state in 1861 when the regional states of the peninsula, along with Sardinia and Sicily, were united under King Victor Emmanuel II. An era of parliamentary government came to a close in the early 1920s when Benito Mussolini established a Fascist dictatorship. His alliance with Nazi Germany led to Italy's defeat in World War II.

A democratic republic replaced the monarchy in 1946 and economic revival followed. Italy is a charter member of NATO and the European Economic Community, and its subsequent successors the EC and the EU. It has been at the forefront of European economic and political unification, joining the Economic and Monetary Union in 1999.

Nonetheless, Italy faces its own set of challenges. Persistent problems include:

  • Sluggish economic growth
  • High youth and female unemployment
  • Organized crime
  • Corruption
  • Economic disparities between southern Italy and the more prosperous north

Natural Hazards in Italy

Regional risks in Italy include:

  • Landslides
  • Mudflows
  • Avalanches
  • Corruption
  • Earthquakes
  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Flooding
  • Land subsidence (particularly in Venice)

Out of all of these natural hazards, volcanism (volcanic activity) is deemed significant in Italy. Etna, which erupted in 2013, is Europe's most actice volcano. Vesuvious, another volcano, remains a threat to the millions of nearby residents in the Bay of Naples area. Both Etna and Vesuvius have been deemed 'Decade Volcanoes' by the International Association of Volcanology and Chemistry of the Earth's Interior.

Other historically active volcanoes include:

  • Stromboli
  • Campi Flegrei
  • Ischia
  • Larderello
  • Pantelleria
  • Vulcano
  • Vulsini

Population Distribution

Despite a distinctive pattern with an industrial north and an agrarian south, a fairly even population distribution exists throughout most of the country, with coastal areas, the Po River Valley, and urban centers (particularly Milan, Rome, and Naples), attracting larger and denser populations.

Italy's Government

The Executive Branch

Italy is a parliamentary republic, with its government divided into three distinct branches (the executive, legislative, and judicial branches). In particular, the executive branch is divided further into specific roles, functions, and appointment and election rules.

Chief of State

The chief of state, also known as the president, is a key role under the executive branch. The current chief of state is President Sergio Mattarella since 3 February 2015.

Heads of Government

Other important roles within the executive branch include the prime minister and deputy prime ministers. The current head of government is Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, since 22 October 2022. The prime minister's official title is President of the Council of Ministers. The current deputy prime ministers are Matteo Salvini and Antonio Taiani, appointed on 22 October 2022 as well. Deputy prime ministers are known officially as Vice Presidents of the Council of Ministers.

Cabinet

The cabinet also falls under the executive branch. The cabinet is comprised of the Council of Ministers, a body proposed by the prime minister. Cabinet members are nominated by the president.

Elections and Appointments

Within the executive branch, the president is indirectly elected by an electoral college consisting of 58 regional representatives for a seven year term (no term limits). The last election was held between 24 January 2022 and 26 January 2022. The prime minister is appointed by the president and confirmed by parliament.

Election Results

Sergio Mattarella was elected president for the first time in 2015. He ran as an independent candidate, winning the electoral college vote count in the fourth round with 665 out of 995 votes (505 vote threshold). He was then re-elected as president in 2022, winning the electoral college vote count in the eighth round with 759 out of 1,009 votes.

Flag Description

The Italian flag is designed with three vertical bands of green (hoist side), white, and red. The design is inspired by the French flag brought to Italy by Napolean in 1797. The colors are those of Milan (red and white) combined with the green uniform color of the Milanese civic guard.
