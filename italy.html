Introduction to Italy

Italy became a nation-state in 1861 when the regional states of the peninsula, along with Sardinia and Sicily, were united under King Victor Emmanuel II. An era of parliamentary government came to a close in the early 1920s when Benito Mussolini established a Fascist dictatorship. His alliance with Nazi Germany led to Italy's defeat in World War II.

A democratic republic replaced the monarchy in 1946 and economic revival followed. Italy is a charter member of NATO and the European Economic Community (EEC) and its subsequent successors the EC and the EU. It has been at the forefront of European economic and political unification, joining the Economic and Monetary Union in 1999.

Nontheless, Italy faces its own set of challenges. Persistent problems include:

  • Sluggish economic growth
  • High youth and female unemployment
  • Organized crime
  • Corruption
  • Economic disparities between southern Italy and the more prosperous north

Italy's Geography

Location

Italy is located in southern Europe, a penninsula extending into the central Mediterranean Sea, northeast of Tunisia.

Italy's Climate

Italy has a predominately Mediterranean climate. It is alpine in the far north, hot and dry in the south.

For more information on Italy's climate, visit Climate of Italy

Italy's Terrain

Italy is mostly rugged and mountainous with some plains on the coastal lowlands.

Natural Hazards in Italy

Regional risks in Italy include:

  • Landslides
  • Mudflows
  • Avalanches
  • Corruption
  • Earthquakes
  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Flooding
  • Land subsidence (particularly in Venice)

People and Society of Italy

Languages

Italy is home to a variety of languages, including:

  • Italian (official)
  • German (parts of Trentino-Alto Adige region)
  • French (Valle d'Aosta region)
  • Slovene (Trieste-Gorizia region)

Population Distribution

Despite a distinctive pattern with an industrial north and an agrarian south, a fairly even population distribution exists throughout most of the country, with coastal areas, the Po River Valley, and urban centers (particularly Milan, Rome, and Naples), attracting larger and denser populations.

The Italian Government

Government type

Italy is a parliamentary republic, with its capital located in Rome.

Administrative divisions

Italy is divided administratively into regions. There are 15 singular regions and five autonomous regions. The 15 singular regions are:

  1. Abruzzo
  2. Basilicata
  3. Calabria
  4. Campania
  5. Emilia-Romagna
  6. Lazio
  7. Liguria
  8. Lombardia
  9. Marche
  10. Molise
  11. Piemonte
  12. Puglia
  13. Toscana
  14. Umbria
  15. Veneto

The five autonomous regions are:

  1. Friuli Venezia Giulia
  2. Sardegna
  3. Sicilia
  4. Trentino-Alto Adige
  5. Valle d'Aosta

Flag Description

The Italian flag is designed with three vertical bands of green (hoist side), white, and red. The design is inspired by the French flag brought to Italy by Napolean in 1797. The colors are those of Milan (red and white) combined with the green uniform color of the Milanese civic guard.
